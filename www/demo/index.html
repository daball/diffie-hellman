1. Diffie-Hellman Key Exchange


  • The Diffie-Hellman algorithm provides a mathematical method for the secure exchange of public keys.
  • It is generally safe against eavesdropping as long as the group of prime numbers is large and the random prime numbers are sufficiently random.

2. How Diffie-Hellman Works

Consider Alice and Bob, two parties who want to establish a secret key to communicate securely:

  1. Shared primes: Alice and Bob decide upon two large, prime numbers, to use as the base, q and modulus, p.
    p (modulus) = random large prime number
    q (base) = random large prime number
  2. Secret: Alice and Bob decide upon one large integer each.
    a (Alice's private key) = random large integer
    b (Bob's private key) = random large integer
  3. Public keys: Once Alice has a, p, and q and Bob has b, p, and q, they can share their public keys, A and B.
    A (Alice's public key) = qa mod p
    B (Bob's public key) = qb mod p
  4. Secret key: Alice and Bob can then each calculate the shared session key to use for encryption purposes.
    s (shared computation) = Ba mod p = Ab mod p

3. What Diffie-Hellman Is Not

  • Diffie-Hellman is not an encryption algorithm. You can use the session key generated to develop a secret key for well-established symmetric cryptograhic algorithms.
  • Diffie-Hellman does not provide authentication of either party to either party.
  • Diffie-Hellman does not protect against a Man-In-The-Middle (MITM) attack. A skillful attacker can connect to each party on behalf of the other and intercept and/or modify plaintext messages exchanged in a compromised Diffie-Hellman session.
